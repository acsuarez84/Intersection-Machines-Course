Before you start

A note on making as argument

Before this assignment starts, you already know that making something can be a form of scholarly argument, not just a decoration. This came from Unit 3's short reading and discussion on "critical making," alongside the pattern-finding work in your prompt journal. Click below for the recap.

What that earlier lesson gave you

Reading: Ratto, "Critical Making: Conceptual and Material Studies in Technology and Social Life."

It gave you two things: the words to explain why the way something is made can carry meaning — why a rough, handmade zine might argue something a polished website cannot, or why a broken link might be part of the point — and one low-stakes practice round, a small "argument sketch" in a medium of your choice. So this is not the first time you've turned an idea into something you can hold or click through. If this lesson hasn't happened yet this semester, it needs to before this assignment launches.

New for this project

Readings for this project

These three readings are new for Unit 4 — short, undergraduate-level, and pulled directly from UCF's Digital Humanities Research Guide, "Access, Equity & Ethics" section, so you're working from texts the field has already vetted as a starting point. Each is linked to its real source and pairs with a specific part of the project below.

📖 Williams, "Disability, Universal Design, and the Digital Humanities" (2012)

Open access. Short and plainly written — makes the case that inaccessible design is a choice, not a neutral default. Useful if disability is your identity marker.

📖 Gitelman & Jackson, Introduction to "Raw Data" Is an Oxymoron (2013)

Its core claim — that data is always constructed, never neutral or "raw" — is the theoretical backbone of Unit 1's idea that AI's design is not neutral.

📖 Keralis, "Disrupting Labor in Digital Humanities; or, The Classroom Is Not Your Crowd" (2018)

Open access. Reframes whose labor gets erased inside digital systems — worth carrying into your reflection when you think about the human labor behind the AI tool you're studying.

Two decisions, not one

The role of AI in this assignment

All semester, you have critiqued AI for how it affects and shapes identity. This assignment does not let you avoid AI completely — but it also does not require you to use it yourself. Even students who never prompt a system themselves have been recording what they observe about AI all semester, and that counts as real engagement.

Students sometimes read "do you use AI or not" as one yes/no choice. It's actually two independent decisions — click each option below to see what it means. Neither choice affects your grade.

Decision A — Research method (Part 1)

Prompt it myself: log your own prompts and outputs in real time, all semester, in your prompt journal.

Decision B — Production method (Part 2)

AI helps build it: use AI tools for layout, starter code, or image generation — log every use in your journal.

Use disclaimer

AI is a core part of this course because it is the thing we are critiquing and working with directly. The following rules apply to anyone who uses AI at any stage:

✓ AI is permitted for

  • Being the subject of your critique — the AI system you're studying, or something your artifact responds to.
  • Helping design or build your artifact (layout, starter code, images) — log every use.
  • Brainstorming ideas — only under the rules below.

✕ AI is NOT permitted for

  • Replacing your own thinking and your own decisions in your writing and design.
  • Writing any part of your written responses for you — your journal analysis and final reflection must be in your own words.
The rules for using AI to brainstorm ideas

"Brainstorming with teacher permission" is not an open door. Before you can ask an AI tool for artifact ideas, you must already have written your own list of at least three ideas, dated and logged. Only after that can you ask AI for suggestions — and you need instructor permission first, so come to office hours or send an email before you do it.

Every idea an AI gives you goes into your prompt journal word-for-word, and your final reflection must include a short note on where your ideas came from: which ones were yours, which came from AI, and — in your own words, not the AI's — why you kept, combined, or dropped each one. If you can't explain your own reasoning for an idea, that's a sign the idea isn't really yours yet.

The big question

What is the assignment

All semester, we have been asking one question: how does AI shape who we are? Identity Construction Through AI is where you answer that question directly. You will closely study a generative AI tool of your choice (or one recommended in the syllabus), focused on one identity marker of your choosing — race, gender, language, class, disability, and so on — and back up what you find with real evidence.

Your prompt journal is where this pays off: you already have a dated collection of prompts and output analysis you've built all semester. Your final delivery is a creative, multimedia artifact, along with a written reflection that explains your methods, findings, and what you want your audience to understand.

Learning objectives

🔍 Analysis

Apply feminist and intersectionality frameworks to explain how a generative AI system represents an identity marker, and why that representation matters.

🧪 Method

Design and carry out a systematic, documented study of an AI tool — a repeatable research practice, not casual prompting — whether you run it yourself or compile published evidence.

🎨 Making

Produce an original artifact that argues something through its form, applying the critical-making principles from Unit 3.

🪞 Transparency

Reflect honestly on the role AI played in your own process, and articulate what you learned about what it can and can't do.

Three parts

The work

Your prompt journal is the foundation this whole assignment is built on. Beginning in Week 1, you have been recording: (1) the exact prompts you submitted, (2) the outputs those tools produced, and (3) your analysis of what changed, what was lost, and what was rebuilt differently. By the end of the semester your journal is a personal collection of evidence. Checkpoints are due Weeks 4, 8, and 12, with real, dated entries — not a batch written the night before.

If you've been observing instead of prompting AI yourself

Some of you have used your journal all semester to record what you observed about AI — demonstrations in class, examples brought in from outside sources — rather than prompting systems yourselves, because of your own values. That is a complete, equal way of doing this project, not a lesser one.

For your Unit 4 project, gather at least fifteen documented AI outputs related to your identity marker from at least two sources you can cite — logged in-class demonstrations, published studies (e.g. Buolamwini and Gebru's Gender Shades study), news investigations, or a classmate's journal shared with permission. Write down the original source, date, and context for each one. This fully satisfies Part 1 and is graded on the same rubric as the direct-prompting path.

Your artifact is a creative, multimedia response to what you found in your prompt journal. It should do three things: make an argument, not just describe what you found; use its form on purpose, so the way it looks and works reflects your critique; and be something an audience outside the classroom could understand.

How this is graded — skill is not the point

None of the options below assume you already know how to build them, and none are graded on how polished they look. A hand-drawn, photocopied zine and a professionally coded website can earn the same grade if the argument and the thoughtfulness of the choices are equally strong. Workshop time for each medium is built into the last two weeks of Unit 4 — but even without it, the simplest version of any option earns full credit.

📓 Zine digital or physical

Long used in feminist and activist communities to reclaim narratives, so the medium itself can be part of your critique. If physical, photograph your process and product for Canvas.

🌐 Website

Build it yourself or use AI to help with design. Treat your design choices — including anywhere you used AI — as part of the argument itself.

🗂️ GitHub

A repository of files the public can see. Works well with Claude Code for turning design ideas into something real.

💬 Something else

Email me or book office hours to propose a different medium before you start.

Choosing not to use AI

Every option above can be built entirely without AI tools — a hand-drawn zine, a hand-coded website, a GitHub repo with no AI-generated files. There is no separate "AI-free track." Whichever medium you pick, Decision B above is what determines whether AI touches your production process, and your reflection is where you explain the creative decisions either way. Graded the same either way.

Your reflection (500–800 words) explains your work as a scholar: the how and why behind your argument, your artifact design, and your choice of identity marker. Check off each part as you draft it:

Grading criteria

This is a small, discussion-driven course rather than a large intro lecture, so grading here is a flexible, criteria-based rubric rather than a rigid point scale — the strongest submissions won't all look alike, especially across artifact types. Weights are listed for transparency; grading within each row is holistic.

CriterionWeightWhat strong work looks like
Journal & evidence 25%
  • Real, dated entries at all three checkpoints — not reconstructed the night before
  • Either 15+ controlled prompts across two AI systems, or (alternate path) 15+ compiled outputs from two citable sources
  • Outputs logged verbatim, with your own analysis of what changed, what was lost, what was rebuilt
Argument & frameworks 25%
  • A named feminist or intersectionality framework doing real analytical work, not a passing citation
  • At least one Unit 4 reading (Williams, Gitelman & Jackson, or Keralis) in real conversation with your findings
  • Claims are specific to your own evidence, not general statements about "how AI is"
The artifact 30%
  • Makes an argument through its form — not a summary of your journal in another format
  • Design and medium choices are intentional and explainable
  • Understandable to an audience outside the classroom
  • Graded on argument and intentionality, never on technical polish
Reflection & disclosure 20%
  • All six reflection points addressed in 500–800 words
  • A complete AI disclosure, with links and transcripts
  • An honest idea-provenance note if AI was used for brainstorming

For my professor

A note on how my vision for the course changed

Earlier drafts of this assignment treated "use AI or don't" as one switch, with a single AI-free essay option carrying the whole weight of that choice. Splitting it into two independent decisions — research method and production method — came directly out of your feedback, and it's a better fit for how students actually feel about these tools: someone can be fully willing to study an AI system while still refusing to let one touch their own creative work, or the reverse.

I also tightened the brainstorming permission into an actual guardrail after your note about offloading risk — students now have to show their own ideas first, on the record, before AI enters the room at all. And I made the grading promise explicit rather than implied: none of the artifact options assume prior technical skill, and craft was never meant to be the thing graded, but earlier drafts left that unstated. It isn't anymore.

Removing the two essay-based options was a smaller edit, made once the two-decision framework existed — they'd become redundant with what Zine, Website, and GitHub can already do, and consolidating kept the choice architecture cleaner for students to actually use.
