Two decisions, not one

The role of AI in this assignment

All semester, you have critiqued AI for how it affects and shapes identity. This assignment does not let you avoid AI completely — but it also does not require you to use it yourself. Even students who never prompt a system themselves have been recording what they observe about AI all semester, and that counts as real engagement.

Students sometimes read "do you use AI or not" as one yes/no choice. It's actually two independent decisions — click each option below to see what it means. Neither choice affects your grade.

Decision A — Research method (Part 1)

Prompt it myself: log your own prompts and outputs in real time, all semester, in your prompt journal.
Which systems you can use

Part 1 asks for controlled prompts across at least two AI systems, and "two systems" can mean two different kinds of engine, not just two brands of the same thing — varying the type of system often surfaces different patterns than varying the brand alone. A few starting points:

  • Text / chat models. Claude and ChatGPT describing "a scientist" with no gender specified, to compare default word choice; Claude and Gemini asked to write a short bio for the same disability marker, to compare tone.
  • Image generators. Midjourney and ChatGPT's built-in image generator (OpenAI retired DALL-E in May 2026; image generation now lives inside ChatGPT itself) generating "a CEO" across varied race and gender prompts, to compare who gets rendered by default; Stable Diffusion and Midjourney generating "a nurse who uses a wheelchair," to compare how each visualizes assistive devices.
  • Voice and audio AI. Two voice assistants (for example Siri and Alexa) responding to the same accented speech input, to compare transcription accuracy; two text-to-speech tools reading the same name aloud, to compare pronunciation handling.
  • Translation tools. Google Translate and DeepL translating a gendered sentence from Spanish to English, to compare how each resolves ambiguous gender; two translation tools handling a disability-related idiom, to compare literalness versus context.

Whichever pairing you choose, keep the rest of the protocol the same: identical prompts across both systems, everything logged verbatim and dated.

Decision B — Production method (Part 2)

AI helps build it: use AI tools for layout, starter code, or image generation — log every use in your journal.
Where AI-free source material can come from

Choosing "Build it AI-free" doesn't mean your artifact has to come from nowhere. Instead of responding to AI-generated prompts and outputs, you can build your artifact in response to real-world material you observe and analyze the same way — treating it as your "prompt" the way an AI output would be. A few starting points:

  • Social media posts. Comment threads on a viral post about a disabled creator; an Instagram algorithm's pattern of what gets promoted versus buried for a given identity marker; a TikTok trend's language around race or gender.
  • What you see in the media. How a news outlet captions photos of women in a specific profession; casting patterns in a TV show's depiction of a disability; a magazine's language across decades for the same identity marker.
  • A body of work by an author. Recurring language patterns across a novelist's female characters; how a poet's collected work represents disability over time; a journalist's consistent framing of a racial group across their published articles.
  • A scholarly journal. How a field's peer-reviewed literature has described a group's contributions across a set of published articles; citation patterns that include or exclude certain authors' identities; language shifts in a journal's coverage of a topic over several issues.

Whichever source you choose, the same rigor from Part 1 applies: document it systematically, date your observations, and analyze the pattern rather than just describing one example.

Use disclaimer

AI is a core part of this course because it is the thing we are critiquing and working with directly. The following rules apply to anyone who uses AI at any stage:

AI is permitted for

  • Being the subject of your critique — the AI system you're studying, or something your artifact responds to.
  • Helping design or build your artifact (layout, starter code, images) — log every use.
  • Brainstorming ideas — only under the rules below.

AI is NOT permitted for

  • Replacing your own thinking and your own decisions in your writing and design.
  • Writing any part of your written responses for you — your journal analysis and final reflection must be in your own words.
The rules for using AI to brainstorm ideas

"Brainstorming with teacher permission" is not an open door. Before you can ask an AI tool for artifact ideas, you must already have written your own list of at least three ideas, dated and logged. Only after that can you ask AI for suggestions — and you need instructor permission first, so come to office hours or send an email before you do it.

Every idea an AI gives you goes into your prompt journal word-for-word, and your final reflection must include a short note on where your ideas came from: which ones were yours, which came from AI, and — in your own words, not the AI's — why you kept, combined, or dropped each one. If you can't explain your own reasoning for an idea, that's a sign the idea isn't really yours yet.

The big question

What is the assignment

All semester, we have been asking one question: how does AI shape who we are? Identity Construction Through AI is where you answer that question directly. You will closely study a generative AI tool of your choice (or one recommended in the syllabus), focused on one identity marker of your choosing — race, gender, language, class, disability, and so on — and back up what you find with real evidence.

Your prompt journal is where this pays off: you already have a dated collection of prompts and output analysis you've built all semester. Your final delivery is a creative, multimedia artifact, along with a written reflection that explains your methods, findings, and what you want your audience to understand.

Learning objectives

Analysis

Apply feminist and intersectionality frameworks to explain how a generative AI system represents an identity marker, and why that representation matters.

Method

Design and carry out a systematic, documented study of an AI tool — a repeatable research practice, not casual prompting — whether you run it yourself or compile published evidence.

Making

Produce an original artifact that argues something through its form, applying the critical-making principles from Unit 3.

Transparency

Reflect honestly on the role AI played in your own process, and articulate what you learned about what it can and can't do.

Three parts

The work

Your prompt journal is the foundation this whole assignment is built on. Beginning in Week 1, you have been recording: (1) the exact prompts you submitted, (2) the outputs those tools produced, and (3) your analysis of what changed, what was lost, and what was rebuilt differently. By the end of the semester your journal is a personal collection of evidence. Checkpoints are due Weeks 4, 8, and 12, with real, dated entries — not a batch written the night before.

If you've been observing instead of prompting AI yourself

Some of you have used your journal all semester to record what you observed about AI — demonstrations in class, examples brought in from outside sources — rather than prompting systems yourselves, because of your own values. That is a complete, equal way of doing this project, not a lesser one.

For your Unit 4 project, gather at least fifteen documented AI outputs related to your identity marker from at least two sources you can cite — logged in-class demonstrations, published studies (e.g. Buolamwini and Gebru's Gender Shades study), news investigations, or a classmate's journal shared with permission. Write down the original source, date, and context for each one. This fully satisfies Part 1 and is graded on the same rubric as the direct-prompting path.

Your artifact is a creative, multimedia response to what you found in your prompt journal. It should do three things: make an argument, not just describe what you found; use its form on purpose, so the way it looks and works reflects your critique; and be something an audience outside the classroom could understand.

How this is graded — skill is not the point

None of the options below assume you already know how to build them, and none are graded on how polished they look. A hand-drawn, photocopied zine and a professionally coded website can earn the same grade if the argument and the thoughtfulness of the choices are equally strong. Workshop time for each medium is built into the last two weeks of Unit 4 — but even without it, the simplest version of any option earns full credit.

Zine digital or physical

Long used in feminist and activist communities to reclaim narratives, so the medium itself can be part of your critique. If physical, photograph your process and product for Canvas. Other digital or physical formats are welcome too — a short film, a photo essay, a piece of physical craft, a podcast episode, or a written essay (10 pages, double-spaced, 12-point font) — as long as the medium is doing real argumentative work.

Programs you can use, free, online: Canva (zine templates) and Adobe Express (zine templates).

Choosing not to use AI

The zine (or any other digital/physical format, including the essay) can be built entirely without AI tools — a hand-drawn zine, a typed essay, a hand-shot short film. The AI artifact option is inherently AI-assisted production, by definition. For every other medium, Decision B above is what determines whether AI touches your process, and your reflection is where you explain the creative decisions either way. Grading criteria

This is a small, discussion-driven course rather than a large intro lecture, so grading here is a flexible, criteria-based rubric rather than a rigid point scale — the strongest submissions won't all look alike, especially across artifact types. This assignment is scored out of 100 points total; that score is what counts for 40% of your final grade. Each criterion below is broken into point bands — click any row for a concrete example of what work at that level actually looks like.

All 16 examples below trace one running case study, so you can see the same project scored differently criterion by criterion: a student auditing how an AI system describes "a nurse" when two identity markers — gender and disability — are combined in the prompt.

These examples are illustrations, not templates

Nothing shown in these popups — the prompts, the responses, the reflection excerpts, the disclosure language — may be copied or submitted as your own work in any part of this project. They exist to show you what a given point level looks like in practice, not to hand you finished material. If your submission echoes this example's specific wording, it will be treated as not your own work.

The nursing context itself is a different matter: if you want to study how AI systems represent nurses as your actual identity-marker topic, that is allowed, but only with prior approval from your professor during Unit 1. Approval for the topic does not extend to reusing any of the specific language shown here.

If you chose not to use AI at any stage

Every band below applies to you exactly as written — there is no separate, lesser rubric for the alternate paths. If you compiled published outputs instead of prompting AI yourself (Part 1), your evidence is scored on the same completeness and citation standard as a prompt log. If you built your artifact entirely AI-free (Part 2), it is scored on the same argument-and-intentionality standard as one where AI helped, and simplicity of build never costs you points. Your reflection and disclosure swap an AI-use transcript for a complete source list — the honesty and specificity bar stays the same either way.

For my professor

A note on how my vision for the course changed

Earlier drafts of this assignment treated "use AI or don't" as one switch, with a single AI-free essay option carrying the whole weight of that choice. Splitting it into two independent decisions — research method and production method — came directly out of your feedback, and it's a better fit for how students actually feel about these tools: someone can be fully willing to study an AI system while still refusing to let one touch their own creative work, or the reverse.

I also tightened the brainstorming permission into an actual guardrail after your note about offloading risk — students now have to show their own ideas first, on the record, before AI enters the room at all. And I made the grading promise explicit rather than implied: none of the artifact options assume prior technical skill, and craft was never meant to be the thing graded, but earlier drafts left that unstated. It isn't anymore.

The artifact options themselves went through another round after that: I dropped Website and GitHub, since in practice they were pulling students toward technical polish rather than argument. In their place, the zine option now explicitly covers other digital and physical formats — including a return of the essay, now folded into the zine option rather than standing alone as its own lesser-seeming choice — and I added a dedicated "AI artifact" option for students building directly with a tool like Claude, so that path is named honestly instead of hiding inside "website."
